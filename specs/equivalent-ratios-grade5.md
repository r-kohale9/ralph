# Learning Goal: Equivalent Ratios for Grade 5

## One-Line Goal

Students understand that two ratios are equivalent when both quantities are multiplied (or divided) by the same number, and can generate, identify, and use equivalent ratios in real-world contexts.

---

## Prerequisite

**Ratio Intuition (Grade 5)** — students already understand the "for every" relationship, can scale both sides of a ratio, and can detect when a ratio is broken. This goal builds on that foundation by making equivalence explicit and introducing ratio tables.

---

## What's IN Scope (5 Testable Skills)

### 1. Identify whether two ratios are equivalent

Given two ratios expressed as "for every A, there are B" and "for every C, there are D," the student determines if they represent the same relationship by checking whether both sides were multiplied by the same number.

Example: "For every 2 apples, there are 3 oranges" and "For every 6 apples, there are 9 oranges" — the kid says "Same! Both multiplied by 3."

Example: "For every 1 cup of flour, there are 2 eggs" and "For every 3 cups of flour, there are 5 eggs" — the kid says "Different! Flour tripled but eggs didn't."

### 2. Generate an equivalent ratio by multiplying both sides

Given a ratio and a multiplier, the student produces the equivalent ratio.

Example: "2 red beads for every 5 blue beads. Make it 3 times bigger." → Kid writes 6 red, 15 blue.

Example: "For every 4 wheels, there's 1 car. Double it." → Kid writes 8 wheels, 2 cars.

### 3. Find the missing value in an equivalent ratio pair

Given one complete ratio and a partial second ratio, the student finds the missing number.

Example: 3 : 4 = 9 : ___ → Kid answers 12 (because 3 × 3 = 9, so 4 × 3 = 12).

Example: 2 : 5 = ___ : 15 → Kid answers 6 (because 5 × 3 = 15, so 2 × 3 = 6).

### 4. Complete a row in a ratio table

Given a ratio table with some filled values and one blank cell, the student fills in the missing value by identifying the pattern.

Example:
| Red  | 2 | 4 | 6 | ___ |
| Blue | 3 | 6 | 9 | 12  |

Kid fills in 8 (pattern: red goes up by 2 each time, or red = blue × 2/3).

### 5. Choose the equivalent ratio from options (with distractors)

Given a target ratio, the student picks the equivalent one from 3-4 options where distractors use additive reasoning or swap the order.

Example: Which is equivalent to 3 : 5?
- A) 6 : 10 ✓ (correct — both × 2)
- B) 6 : 8 ✗ (additive — added 3 to each)
- C) 5 : 3 ✗ (swapped order)
- D) 9 : 12 ✗ (only first × 3, second wrong)

---

## What's OUT (Hard Boundary)

- Simplifying ratios to lowest terms (dividing down) — only multiplying up
- Cross-multiplication as a tested procedure
- Fractions, decimals, or percentages as ratio representations
- Unit rate calculations
- Ratios with more than two quantities
- Non-integer multipliers (only whole: ×2, ×3, ×4, ×5)
- Values larger than 50 in any position
- Formal ratio notation like "3:5" as the tested concept — may appear as label, never the point
- Part-to-whole ratios — stays part-to-part

---

## Finiteness Check

Five discrete skills. Each testable in a single interaction. Skill 1 is recognition (same/different), Skills 2-4 are production (generate/find/complete), Skill 5 is selection (MCQ with traps). A student either understands equivalent ratios through multiplication or falls back on additive reasoning.
